Q: What is chilling injury (CI) and when does it occur?
A: Chilling injury is biological damage caused by exposing tropical and subtropical produce to non-freezing temperatures below their specific threshold, typically between 5°C and 12.5°C. It causes membrane phase transition, cellular ion leakage, and enzymatic browning.

Q: What enzyme causes the blackening visible in chilling-injured produce?
A: Polyphenol oxidase (PPO) and peroxidase (POD) escape their cellular compartments when membranes rupture from cold stress, then oxidize phenolic substrates to form brown polymers causing tissue blackening.

Q: What is the chilling injury threshold for bananas, and what happens when they are refrigerated?
A: Bananas chill-injure below 11.5°C to 13°C (about 52–55°F). Refrigeration permanently blackens vascular tissue, halts starch-to-sugar conversion, and leaves the fruit grey, astringent, and unable to ripen even after returning to room temperature.

Q: What is the safe storage temperature for basil, and why can't it go in a standard cooler?
A: Basil must be held at 50°F / 10°C or above. Below that threshold, PPO and lipoxygenase activity causes rapid leaf blackening, necrotic spotting, and epinasty within hours of cold exposure.

Q: What is "cold-induced sweetening" in potatoes, and why does it ruin processing crops?
A: Cold-induced sweetening occurs when potatoes for frying or chipping are stored below 8°C. Low temperatures activate vacuolar invertase, converting stored sucrose into glucose and fructose; when fried, these reducing sugars undergo the Maillard reaction, producing unacceptably dark, bitter products that cannot be reversed by re-warming.

Q: What is the Q10 coefficient, and what does it mean practically for produce storage?
A: Q10 measures the rate change of biological processes per 10°C temperature increase. For most produce it is 2.0–2.5, meaning every 10°C rise more than doubles respiration rate and cuts shelf life proportionally. Highly perishable crops can reach Q10 values of 4.0–5.0.

Q: What relative humidity should leafy greens and most vegetables be stored at?
A: Most leafy greens require 95–100% relative humidity to prevent transpirational water loss, cellular plasmolysis, wilting, and shriveling.

Q: Why should dry onions and garlic never be stored in high humidity?
A: RH above 75% immediately triggers adventitious root growth in onions and promotes Botrytis neck rot. Garlic stored between 40–50°F develops aggressive internal sprout elongation, making cloves hollow and unmarketable.

Q: What ethylene concentration can cause serious damage to sensitive crops?
A: As little as 100 parts per billion (ppb) of ethylene can induce severe physiological damage in sensitive commodities.

Q: What does ethylene exposure do to carrots?
A: Ethylene stimulates isocoumarin synthesis in carrots, causing irreversible and intense bitterness.

Q: What are the visible effects of ethylene on lettuce?
A: Ethylene triggers russet spotting — brown pigmentation along the midrib — and accelerates leaf senescence.

Q: What is the ideal storage temperature for spinach, and what is its shelf life?
A: Spinach stores best at 32°F / 0°C with 95–100% RH for 10–14 days. Free water on leaves must be avoided as it accelerates bacterial soft rot.

Q: Why must Belgian endive (witloof) be kept away from light in storage?
A: Light exposure induces rapid greening and causes extreme bitterness in Belgian endive, destroying its commercial value.

Q: What is the freeze point risk for endive/escarole, and why is it significant?
A: Endive freezes rapidly at –0.1°C (31.7°F), just one-tenth of a degree below the optimal 0°C holding temperature, requiring very precise temperature control.

Q: What storage temperature and humidity does broccoli require, and what destroys it?
A: Broccoli needs 32°F / 0°C at 95–100% RH with a shelf life of 10–14 days. Warm storage and ethylene exposure trigger floret (bead) yellowing and loosening, which completely terminates marketability.

Q: Why does cauliflower need to be protected from light in storage?
A: Light induces unwanted greening of the cauliflower curd; ethylene additionally triggers premature leaf abscission.

Q: What happens to cabbage when relative humidity drops in storage?
A: Outer leaves suffer extreme papery desiccation, ethylene causes leaf abscission, and the green color is lost. Mature cabbage can store 3–6 months at 32°F / 0°C with 98–100% RH.

Q: What is the correct storage temperature for table potatoes, and what are the two main risks?
A: Table potatoes store at 40–46°F / 4–8°C with 95–98% RH. Temperatures below 4°C (38°F) cause cold-induced sweetening, and light exposure causes toxic solanine greening.

Q: What is the chilling injury threshold for sweet potatoes, and what are the symptoms?
A: Sweet potatoes chill-injure below 13°C (55°F). Refrigeration causes the core to become hard with mahogany browning, the flesh fails to soften when cooked, and the tuber rots rapidly. They must be held at 55–59°F / 13–15°C.

Q: What happens to carrots stored near ethylene-producing items like apples?
A: Ethylene exposure triggers irreversible isocoumarin production, making carrots intensely bitter.

Q: Why must radishes have their tops removed before retail storage?
A: The foliage continues transpiring heavily through the leaves, rapidly drawing moisture out of the root and making it pithy, spongy, and unmarketable.

Q: How long can horseradish be stored, and at what temperature does freeze damage occur?
A: Horseradish stores 10–12 months at 30–32°F / –1 to 0°C with 98–100% RH. Permanent cellular freeze damage occurs below –1.8°C (28.7°F).

Q: What is the "killing zone" for peaches and nectarines, and what does it cause?
A: The killing zone is 36–50°F / 3–10°C. Storage in this range causes severe chilling injury: internal breakdown, woolliness, and profound flavor loss. They must be held at 31–32°F / –0.5 to 0°C or allowed to ripen at room temperature.

Q: What is the same killing zone temperature range for plums and apricots?
A: Both suffer internal browning, flesh translucency, and chilling injury between 3–10°C, the same killing zone as peaches. Store at 31–32°F / –0.5 to 0°C.

Q: How do you know sweet cherries are poorly stored, and what causes it?
A: Brown, shriveled, brittle stems (pedicels) indicate dehydration from delayed cooling. Sweet cherries store at 30–32°F / –1 to 0°C with 90–95% RH for 2–3 weeks.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for fresh olives, and at what temperature should they be stored?
A: Fresh olives chill-injure below 5°C and should be held at 41–50°F / 5–10°C with 85–90% RH for 4–6 weeks.

Q: Why are apples a serious hazard in a mixed produce cooler?
A: Apples are very high ethylene producers, generating enough ethylene to destroy ethylene-sensitive vegetables stored in the same cooler. They also become mealy when overripe due to cellular collapse.

Q: Which apple cultivars are chilling-sensitive, and at what temperature do they suffer injury?
A: McIntosh and Yellow Newtown apples suffer internal browning, soft scald, and core flush if stored at 32°F standard refrigeration. They require 40°F / 4°C.

Q: What is the correct storage temperature for European pears, and what is the risk of warm storage?
A: European pears need 29–31°F / –1.5 to –0.5°C with 90–95% RH. Warm storage rapidly converts starches to sugar and turns the fruit to mush.

Q: What is oleocellosis, and which citrus fruits are most prone to it?
A: Oleocellosis is the rupturing of essential oil glands in the rind from physical stress or chilling, creating toxic oil spotting that increases decay susceptibility. Oranges are particularly prone; it is worsened by rough handling of cold fruit.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for lemons, and what are CI symptoms?
A: Lemons chill-injure below 10–11°C. Symptoms include severe surface pitting, membranous staining, and red blotch. Store lemons at 50–55°F / 10–13°C with 85–90% RH.

Q: What temperature damages limes, and what are the visible symptoms?
A: Storage below 7°C causes deep surface pitting on limes, with skin rapidly turning a necrotic tan/brown. Store limes at 48–50°F / 9–10°C.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for grapefruit, and how cold-sensitive is it?
A: Grapefruit is extremely cold-sensitive with a CI threshold of 10°C (50°F). Store at 50–59°F / 10–15°C. Sub-threshold storage causes scald, extensive pitting, and watery breakdown of the albedo (white pith).

Q: What are the correct storage conditions for mangoes, and what does chilling cause?
A: Mangoes store at 55°F / 13°C with 85–90% RH. Chilling below 10–13°C triggers grayish skin discoloration, lenticel spotting, uneven ripening, failure to color, and internal browning with flavor loss.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for papayas, and what pathogen becomes a risk after CI?
A: Papayas chill-injure below 7°C (45°F). Cold storage causes surface pitting, hard flesh areas, ripening failure, and extreme susceptibility to Alternaria rot. Store at 45–55°F / 7–13°C.

Q: What causes "endogenous brown spot" in pineapples?
A: Chilling injury below 7–10°C causes endogenous brown spot — internal browning — and a dull green exterior color upon return to ambient temperatures. Store pineapples at 45–55°F / 7–13°C.

Q: What are the CI symptoms of avocados, and why is ethylene also a concern?
A: Chilling below 4.5–13°C causes grayish-brown discoloration of the mesocarp flesh. Avocados are also highly sensitive to ethylene, which triggers rapid mushiness. Store at 37–45°F / 3–7°C.

Q: What is the most important rule for handling strawberries after harvest?
A: Do not wash strawberries before storage — washing introduces free water that guarantees rapid Botrytis cinerea proliferation. Even 1 hour at 68°F accelerates decay. Store at 32°F / 0°C with 90–95% RH.

Q: What does temperature fluctuation do to blueberries?
A: Moisture condensation from temperature swings destroys the protective natural wax bloom on blueberry skin and encourages rapid fungal decay. Store at 31–32°F / –0.5 to 0°C.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for cranberries, and what are CI symptoms?
A: Cranberries chill-injure below 2°C (35°F). Symptoms include rubbery texture, dull appearance, and deep red flesh discoloration. Store at 35–41°F / 2–5°C with 90–95% RH.

Q: Why are blackberries and raspberries the most perishable berries?
A: They have no protective cuticle, and even minor mechanical abrasion or harvesting wet causes instantaneous cellular leakage and pathogenic destruction. Shelf life is only 3–6 days at 31–32°F / –0.5 to 0°C.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for cantaloupe, and why is it dangerous in a mixed cooler?
A: Cantaloupe chills below 2–5°C, causing surface pitting and flavor loss. It also produces massive volumes of ethylene, destroying sensitive crops stored nearby. Hold at 36–41°F / 2–5°C.

Q: What does ethylene do to watermelon?
A: Ethylene causes profound loss of internal firmness in watermelon, resulting in a mealy, degraded texture and accelerated flesh breakdown. Store at 50–59°F / 10–15°C with 90% RH.

Q: What humidity do winter squash and pumpkins require, and why is this the opposite of most produce?
A: Winter squash and pumpkins need only 50–70% RH, much lower than most produce. High humidity causes rapid fungal rot (Fusarium, Botrytis) on the hard rind. Store at 54–59°F / 12–15°C.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for cucumbers, and what are the CI symptoms?
A: Cucumbers chill-injure below 7–10°C. Chilling causes water-soaked spots, extreme pitting, and accelerated decay. Ethylene additionally causes rapid irreversible yellowing. Store at 50–54°F / 10–12°C.

Q: What does refrigerating tomatoes do to their flavor?
A: Refrigeration induces epigenetic DNA methylation changes that permanently downregulate volatile synthesis genes, resulting in flavorless, mealy fruit with no chance of flavor recovery. Mature green tomatoes need 50–55°F / 10–13°C.

Q: What are the CI symptoms of bell peppers?
A: Chilling below 7–10°C induces sheet pitting on the surface, Alternaria rot on pods and calyx, and internal darkening of the seeds. Store at 45–50°F / 7–10°C with 95–98% RH.

Q: What does ethylene do to eggplant?
A: Ethylene causes calyx discoloration and abscission in eggplant. Chilling independently causes surface scald and browning of flesh and seeds. Store at 50–54°F / 10–12°C.

Q: What is the Q10 value for mushrooms, and what does this mean for handling?
A: Mushrooms have an extraordinarily high Q10 of 4–5, meaning they deteriorate very rapidly with temperature increases. Any delay in cooling causes rapid browning and cap opening. Free moisture causes slimy bacterial blotch. Store at 32°F / 0°C.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for dragon fruit, and what are the storage symptoms?
A: Dragon fruit chills below 5–6°C. Symptoms include flesh translucency, wilting, darkening of outer scales, flesh browning, and poor flavor. Store at 50–57°F / 10–14°C for 14–24 days.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for jicama, and where should it be stored?
A: Jicama chills below 13°C (55°F). Refrigeration causes severe surface decay, discoloration, and internal browning. Store at 55–65°F / 13–18°C with 85–90% RH.

Q: What happens to asparagus in warm storage or near ethylene sources?
A: Asparagus rapidly lignifies (toughens the spears) if exposed to ethylene or warm temperatures. Chilling below 2°C causes dull, gray-green limp tips. Store at 36°F / 2.5°C with 95–100% RH.

Q: Why must sweet corn be hydrocooled immediately after harvest?
A: Sweet corn has an extraordinarily high Q10 respiration rate. Any failure to cool instantly causes rapid conversion of sugars to starch, permanently ruining sweetness. Shelf life is only 5–8 days at 32°F / 0°C.

Q: What causes cassava (yucca) to develop blue-black vascular streaking, and how is it prevented?
A: Cassava undergoes exceedingly rapid postharvest physiological deterioration that causes blue-black vascular streaking. It must be waxed or cooled immediately after cutting and held at 32–41°F / 0–5°C.

Q: What fumigant is required for table grape storage to prevent Botrytis?
A: Table grapes require sulfur dioxide (SO₂) fumigation to suppress Botrytis cinerea, the primary decay pathogen. Brown, dry stems indicate poor cooling. Store at 31–32°F / –0.5 to 0°C with 90–95% RH for up to 6 months.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for okra, and what are CI symptoms?
A: Okra chills below 7°C (45°F). CI causes severe discoloration, water-soaked areas, pitting, and rapid mold decay. Store at 45–50°F / 7–10°C with 90–95% RH for 7–10 days.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for pomegranates, and what are CI symptoms?
A: Pomegranates chill-injure below 4.5°C (40°F). Symptoms include pitting and both external rind and internal aril browning. Store at 41–45°F / 5–7.2°C with 90–95% RH for 2–3 months.

Q: What is VPD and why does it matter for produce storage?
A: Vapor pressure deficit (VPD) is the difference between the moisture in the air and the moisture the air can hold at saturation. High VPD drives rapid transpirational water loss from plant tissue, leading to wilting, shriveling, and economic loss by weight.

Q: Why is temperature fluctuation during transport dangerous even for non-chilling-sensitive produce?
A: Temperature swings shift the dew point, causing free water condensation on produce surfaces. This provides the exact conditions needed for germination of Botrytis cinerea (gray mold) and bacterial soft rots.

Q: What is the ideal storage temperature and shelf life for garlic, and what temperature range causes sprouting?
A: Garlic stores at 30–32°F / –1 to 0°C with 65–70% RH for 6–7 months. Storing between 40–50°F triggers aggressive internal sprout elongation, making cloves hollow and unmarketable.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for rambutan, and what is the visual sign of poor storage?
A: Rambutan chills below 10°C (50°F). The spinterns (hair-like protrusions) lose moisture almost instantly, turning brown and brittle, making the fruit visually unmarketable. Store at 54°F / 12°C with 90–95% RH.

Q: What is the key storage risk for tomatillos?
A: If the papery husk becomes wet, it harbors aggressive bacterial decay and molds that quickly rot the inner fruit. Store at 45–55°F / 7–13°C with 85–90% RH with dry husks.

Q: What makes passionfruit a hazard in a mixed storage environment?
A: Passionfruit produces massive volumes of ethylene and has a very high ethylene production rate. It requires exceptional ventilation to prevent adjacent commodity damage. Store at 50°F / 10°C with 85–90% RH.

Q: What is the chilling threshold for cherimoya, and what two risks does it pose?
A: Cherimoya chills below 13°C. It suffers profound brown discoloration from cold, and also produces high ethylene, driving extremely rapid over-softening. Store at 55°F / 13°C with 90–95% RH.
