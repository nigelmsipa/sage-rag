Q: What is the difference between a climacteric and a non-climacteric fruit?
A: Climacteric fruits continue to ripen after harvest, converting starches to sugars via ethylene gas production. Non-climacteric fruits stop developing sugars the moment they are severed from the parent plant and will only soften or decay after harvest.

Q: How do you check if a Hass avocado is ripe without bruising it?
A: Press gently at the neck — the narrow top near the stem — not the bulbous bottom. A slight yield to thumb pressure there indicates ripeness.

Q: How long does it take a hard, green avocado to ripen on the counter?
A: About three to four days at room temperature. Placing it in a closed paper bag with an apple or banana speeds the process significantly.

Q: What do brown sugar spots on a banana indicate?
A: They indicate that the starches have fully converted to sugar, making the fruit at peak sweetness, highly digestible, and ideal for eating raw or baking.

Q: How long does a greenish-yellow banana need to fully ripen?
A: About two days at room temperature.

Q: Why is color a poor ripeness indicator for mangoes?
A: Varietal differences make color unreliable — a Tommy Atkins blushes red before it is ripe, an Ataulfo stays yellow, and a Keitt stays entirely green even when ripe.

Q: What are the two reliable ripeness indicators for any mango variety?
A: Softness to a gentle squeeze and a distinct sweet, fruity aroma at the stem end.

Q: How long does a hard mango need to ripen on the counter?
A: Two to three days, until it feels slightly soft and emits a sweet aroma.

Q: What color change signals a ripe peach or nectarine?
A: The background color near the stem shifts from green to yellow or cream.

Q: Where should you press a peach to check ripeness without bruising it?
A: Gently along the vertical seam.

Q: How should stone fruit be positioned on the counter to avoid bruising while ripening?
A: Stored shoulder-down.

Q: How do you check if a kiwi is ripe?
A: Press it gently between thumb and forefinger — it should yield slightly, similar to the firmness of the fleshy base of your thumb.

Q: How long does a firm kiwi take to ripen, and what speeds it up?
A: Three to five days at room temperature; placing it in a fruit bowl next to bananas drastically reduces that time.

Q: What percentage of a papaya's skin should be yellow or orange before it is ready to eat?
A: At least eighty percent.

Q: How long does a mostly green papaya need to ripen?
A: About three days at room temperature until the green fades to deep yellow-orange.

Q: Why should you check a pear's neck rather than its body to test ripeness?
A: Pears ripen from the inside out, so by the time the main body feels soft, the interior core is already overripe and mushy.

Q: How long does a pear with a completely rigid neck need to ripen?
A: About two to three days on the counter.

Q: What is the critical difference between Fuyu and Hachiya persimmons for a customer?
A: Fuyu is non-astringent and eaten crisp like an apple. Hachiya is highly astringent and must be completely gelatinous — like a water balloon — before eating, or it will be intensely bitter.

Q: How long does a hard Hachiya persimmon need to sit before eating?
A: About a week, until it feels completely squishy and the flesh turns translucent.

Q: What visual cue signals that a passion fruit is ripe?
A: The skin becomes heavily wrinkled and dimpled. The more wrinkled the skin, the sweeter and more developed the pulp inside.

Q: Why does leaving a green pineapple on the counter not make it sweeter?
A: Pineapple is non-climacteric — sugar development permanently halts once severed from the plant because the starch reserves in the stem are cut off.

Q: What three indicators confirm a pineapple was harvested at peak maturity?
A: A golden color at the base, a sweet smell at the butt, and a slight give to the shell.

Q: What does a smooth, depressed cavity at the stem end of a cantaloupe indicate?
A: A "full slip" — the melon detached naturally from the vine when fully ripe.

Q: Can a cantaloupe picked with torn stem tissue get sweeter on the counter?
A: No. It will get a bit softer and juicier but will not get any sweeter.

Q: What three signs confirm a watermelon is field-ripe?
A: A creamy yellow field spot on the belly, disproportionate heaviness for its size, and a hollow tenor ring when thumped.

Q: What shape does a ripe pomegranate take, and why?
A: A blocky, somewhat hexagonal shape rather than a perfect sphere, because the internal arils have swelled with juice to maximum capacity and press against the rind.

Q: What is the key indicator that a starfruit has reached full sweetness?
A: Slight browning along the edges of its five ribs, along with an entirely yellow skin.

Q: What physical signs indicate a dragon fruit is ready to eat?
A: An even, slight softness when squeezed and green scale tips that are slightly wilting or browning.

Q: What three sensory cues tell you a whole jackfruit is ripe?
A: The exterior has turned yellowish-brown, the conical spikes have spread apart and flattened slightly, and it emits a powerful musky bubblegum-like aroma.

Q: Why should you oil your knife and hands before cutting a ripe jackfruit?
A: The latex inside is very sticky and will coat hands and blades.

Q: What is the Glycemic Index scale range, and what score qualifies as low GI?
A: The scale runs from 0 to 100; a score of 55 or less is considered low GI.

Q: What is the formula for Glycemic Load?
A: GL = (GI × Carbohydrates in grams) ÷ 100.

Q: What are the GI values for cherries, strawberries, and watermelon?
A: Cherries: 20 (low); strawberries: 41 (low); watermelon: 76 (high).

Q: Which fruits are highly recommended for diabetics based on GI and fiber content?
A: Cherries, apples, strawberries, and peaches — all low GI with good fiber-to-sugar ratios.

Q: Why is watermelon flagged as a caution fruit for diabetics despite having only 11g of carbs per cup?
A: Its GI is very high at 76, meaning it digests and releases sugars very rapidly even though the total carbohydrate load is low.

Q: What simple pairing strategy helps diabetics stabilize blood sugar when eating any fruit?
A: Pairing fruit with a handful of nuts or a protein source to slow digestion.

Q: How many exposures to a new food may a child need before accepting it?
A: Over 18 distinct exposures.

Q: What is a common reason children reject vegetables that is unrelated to flavor?
A: Texture sensitivity — tactile defensiveness to the physical sensation of the food in the mouth.

Q: Name four high-Brix fruits considered "sweetness champions" for picky children.
A: Cotton Candy grapes, Honeycrisp apples, Cara Cara navel oranges, and Sungold kiwi.

Q: What cutting technique helps children accept raw vegetables when texture is the barrier?
A: Julienning into very thin matchsticks, served alongside a familiar dip like hummus or yogurt.

Q: What are the most scientifically supported anti-inflammatory produce items?
A: Dark leafy greens (spinach, kale, collards), tomatoes, allium vegetables (garlic, onions), and vibrantly colored berries (blueberries, strawberries, cherries).

Q: What percentage of Dirty Dozen samples contained detectable pesticide traces according to the EWG?
A: 96%.

Q: What percentage of conventional produce samples fall below EPA safety tolerances according to the USDA Pesticide Data Program?
A: Over 99%.

Q: Name the top five items on the EWG 2026 Dirty Dozen.
A: Strawberries, spinach, kale/collard/mustard greens, grapes, and peaches and nectarines.

Q: Name the top five items on the EWG 2026 Clean Fifteen.
A: Avocados, sweet corn, pineapples, papaya, and onions.

Q: Why should tomatoes never be stored in the refrigerator?
A: Cold temperatures break down cellular walls, inhibit flavor-producing volatile organic compound synthesis, and result in a mealy texture and severe flavor loss.

Q: How should tomatoes be stored at home?
A: At room temperature on the counter, stem-side down to prevent moisture loss from the scar.

Q: Which four fruits are high ethylene producers that should not share a crisper drawer with greens?
A: Apples, bananas, avocados, and peaches.

Q: Why should potatoes not be stored in the refrigerator?
A: Cold triggers an enzymatic reaction that converts starches to sugars, resulting in gritty texture, odd sweetness, and rapid dark caramelization when cooked.

Q: Why must potatoes and onions never be stored together?
A: Onions emit ethylene gas and ambient moisture that breaks down potato dormancy, causing potatoes to sprout and rot rapidly.

Q: Why should berries and greens not be washed before refrigerator storage?
A: Excess moisture introduced during washing catalyzes rapid fungal and bacterial growth.

Q: What setting should the crisper drawer be on for leafy greens, and what setting for apples and grapes?
A: High humidity (vents fully closed) for leafy greens; low humidity (vents open) for rot-prone fruits like apples and grapes.

Q: Why are sealed plastic grocery bags harmful for storing vegetables?
A: Living vegetables continue to respire, and sealing them traps carbon dioxide and moisture, leading to slimy decay and anaerobic bacterial growth.

Q: Why should leafy tops be removed from carrots, radishes, and beets immediately after purchase?
A: The tops continue drawing moisture and nutrients from the taproot, leaving the vegetable limp, rubbery, and dehydrated within days.

Q: What is the three-step method for extending berry shelf life at home?
A: Briefly soak in a mild vinegar-water solution to kill surface spores, spin completely dry in a salad spinner, then store in an airtight container lined with dry paper towels.

Q: How should asparagus and soft herbs like cilantro be stored in the refrigerator?
A: Upright in a glass with an inch of water, loosely covered with a plastic bag to maintain localized humidity — treated like cut flowers.

Q: What does jicama taste like?
A: A cross between a savory apple and a water chestnut — very mild, slightly sweet, and incredibly crisp and juicy.

Q: What is the primary traditional use of mustard greens (sarson) in South Asian cooking?
A: As the cornerstone of the Punjabi winter dish Sarson ka Saag, slow-cooked with spinach and goosefoot, then pureed and thickened with maize flour.

Q: What is the classic French preparation of celeriac?
A: Céleri rémoulade — the raw root is peeled, finely julienned, and dressed in a piquant mustard mayonnaise.

Q: What makes romanesco botanically distinctive?
A: It is an edible flower bud of Brassica oleracea that grows in mathematically perfect logarithmic spirals (fractals).

Q: What flavor does romanesco have compared to broccoli and cauliflower?
A: It sits between the two, but is noticeably nuttier, earthier, and slightly sweeter.

Q: Why is galangal not interchangeable with ginger in Thai recipes?
A: Galangal has a piney, sharp, citrusy, slightly camphor-like flavor and a much denser, woodier structure — it lacks ginger's intense warming heat and cannot serve as an adequate substitute in dishes like Tom Kha Gai.

Q: What are the two uses of shiso leaves distinguished by color?
A: Green leaves are used as edible garnish or wraps for sushi and in cold noodle salads; purple leaves are used to dye and flavor pickled Japanese plums (umeboshi).

Q: Why are ramps only available for a few weeks in spring?
A: They are exclusively foraged in the wild, not cultivated commercially, and their season is extremely brief.

Q: What does the L.A.S.T. de-escalation framework stand for?
A: Listen, Apologize, Solve, Thank.

Q: What physical posture does the Crisis Prevention Institute recommend when de-escalating an upset customer?
A: A relaxed, open body posture, slow movement, hands visible and open (never crossed over the chest), and a consciously lowered volume and cadence of voice.

Q: What causes berries to mold rapidly even when handled correctly at home?
A: A microscopic mold spore from the field can bloom rapidly once the package changes temperature during transport — the store does not use harsh chemical preservatives.
