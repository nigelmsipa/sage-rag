Q: What is the Q₁₀ temperature coefficient, and why does it matter for berry storage?
A: It is the biological principle that a 10°C (18°F) increase in ambient temperature accelerates respiration and enzymatic degradation by 200% to 400%. Any cold chain breakdown immediately triggers explosive fungal growth in berries.

Q: What pathogen causes gray mold, and how does it establish itself before fruit is even harvested?
A: Botrytis cinerea infects floral tissues during bloom and remains dormant as the fruit develops, reactivating only when the fruit ripens and senescence degrades the plant's chemical defenses.

Q: What are the earliest visual signs of gray mold on a strawberry?
A: A pale, light brown, water-soaked lesion near the calyx or at contact points between berries; the affected tissue feels firm and spongy, and decayed tissue cannot be separated from healthy tissue.

Q: What does late-stage gray mold look like?
A: Dense, velvety gray mycelial growth covers the diseased tissue; inside a sealed clamshell, it may appear cottony white before maturing to gray or brown.

Q: Does gray mold produce a noticeable odor?
A: It is largely odorless, though a faint cider-like aroma may develop in severely decayed, heavily sporulating fruit.

Q: At what temperatures does gray mold spread fastest, and how quickly can it move?
A: At 65°F to 75°F, Botrytis can completely consume a berry and spread to multiple surrounding neighbors within 24 to 48 hours.

Q: Why can't you salvage healthy-looking berries from a clamshell with sporulating gray mold?
A: Scraping surface mold does not remove the deep mycelial filaments already penetrating surrounding fruit, nor does it eliminate potential mycotoxins in the tissue.

Q: What must you do to the display rack after pulling a gray mold clamshell?
A: Sanitize the rack, because dry Botrytis spores are easily dislodged and dispersed by the fans in refrigerated display cases.

Q: What does heavy latent Botrytis in an incoming shipment indicate?
A: The cold chain was likely broken during transit, or field conditions prior to harvest were excessively cool and damp.

Q: What disease is called "leak" disease, and which fungus causes it?
A: Rhizopus soft rot, caused by Rhizopus stolonifer and related Mucor species (Zygomycetes).

Q: Which commodities are primarily targeted by Rhizopus soft rot?
A: Strawberries, stone fruit (peaches, nectarines, plums), and cherries.

Q: How does Rhizopus gain entry into fruit?
A: Entirely through wounds, micro-abrasions, or punctures sustained during harvesting and packing — it cannot penetrate intact skin.

Q: What is the late-stage visual signature of Rhizopus soft rot?
A: White, web-like mycelium peppered with black sporangia on hair-like stalks approximately 0.10 inches long; the original fruit is reduced to a liquefied mass resting in its own dissolved cellular fluids.

Q: How quickly can Rhizopus destroy a strawberry, and at what temperature?
A: At temperatures above 65°F, it can reduce a healthy strawberry to a liquefied mass overnight.

Q: Why must you handle a Rhizopus-infected clamshell gently when removing it?
A: Tilting the clamshell can spill pathogen-laden juices onto adjacent healthy product or display surfaces, guaranteeing rapid cross-contamination.

Q: What specialized structure allows Rhizopus to crawl across packaging to adjacent fruit?
A: Rhizoids — root-like vegetative structures that bridge physical gaps between individual berries.

Q: What enzymes does Rhizopus secrete, and what do they destroy in the fruit?
A: Endopolygalacturonase (a pectolytic enzyme) and cellulases, which dismantle the middle lamella binding strawberry cells together, causing the fruit to liquefy.

Q: Why is it unsafe for a consumer to cook Rhizopus-infected fruit into preserves?
A: The pectolytic enzymes are heat-stable — cooking kills the fungus but the enzymes survive and continue dissolving fruit tissues, reducing the product to unpalatable mush.

Q: What food safety threat arises from fluid pooling at the bottom of a Rhizopus-infected clamshell?
A: The nutrient-rich leaked juices create an ideal growth medium for secondary pathogens like Salmonella or Listeria monocytogenes if they are present in the processing environment.

Q: What is the key textural difference between gray mold and blue mold on infected fruit?
A: Gray mold tissue remains firm and tightly bound to healthy tissue; blue mold tissue is fully macerated and separates very easily, sliding out as a wet, mushy cone when pressed.

Q: What pathogen causes downy mildew on grapes, and what class of organism is it?
A: Plasmopara viticola, an obligate biotrophic oomycete (water mold).

Q: How can a clerk visually distinguish downy mildew from powdery mildew on grapes?
A: Downy mildew is distinctly cottony and white; powdery mildew appears as a dry, flour-like gray dusting.

Q: What happens to grape berries with downy mildew when handled?
A: They easily shatter and detach from the rachis (main stem), leaving loose, rotting fruit at the bottom of the bag.

Q: Why doesn't downy mildew spread from berry to berry in a retail cooler?
A: Plasmopara viticola is an obligate biotroph requiring living host tissue — transmission via airborne sporangia and swimming zoospores ceases once the fruit is harvested and chilled.

Q: What does finding sporulating downy mildew on mature retail grapes tell you about the source farm?
A: It indicates a catastrophic failure in the vineyard's early-season fungicide application and canopy management, since grapes normally develop resistance to direct infection approximately 3 to 4 weeks post-bloom.

Q: What bacteria are the primary causes of bacterial soft rot on leafy greens?
A: Pectobacterium carotovorum (formerly Erwinia carotovora), Dickeya dadantii, Pseudomonas marginalis, and Pseudomonas viridiflava.

Q: What enzyme do soft rot bacteria secrete, and what does it attack in the plant?
A: Pectate lyases, which degrade the pectate molecules binding plant cells together, dissolving tissue into a mushy, disorganized mass.

Q: What actually generates the putrid odor of bacterial soft rot — the primary bacteria or something else?
A: Secondary anaerobic bacteria that colonize the liquefied cellular debris produce the putrid odor; the primary Pectobacterium or Pseudomonas pathogens simply dissolve the tissue.

Q: How fast do soft rot bacteria replicate under ideal conditions, and what are those ideal conditions?
A: Every 20 to 60 minutes at 65°F to 95°F; slow but steady degradation continues even under standard refrigeration at 35°F to 40°F.

Q: Why can't you rinse the bacterial slime off adjacent produce to save it?
A: Rinsing only spreads bacteria further into stomata and micro-wounds of healthy leaves; any produce in direct contact with the slime must be discarded as adulterated product.

Q: After a soft rot outbreak on the wet rack, what exactly must be cleaned?
A: The affected section of the rack, including the grates and the underlying drainage pans, must be scrubbed, rinsed, and sanitized with an approved chemical agent before restocking.

Q: What dangerous human pathogen can be sheltered and protected inside a Pseudomonas biofilm in a misting system?
A: Listeria monocytogenes — it integrates into the Pseudomonas EPS matrix and can survive standard sanitation protocols that would easily destroy free-floating Listeria cells.

Q: How does a biofilm-contaminated misting nozzle spread pathogens across the entire wet rack?
A: Water pressure tears microscopic biofilm fragments loose; the nozzles aerosolize these fragments, blanketing the ready-to-eat display in a mist containing viable pathogens.

Q: What technology can actively dismantle biofilms in misting systems without leaving toxic residue on produce?
A: Continuous ozone (O₃) injection systems.

Q: What causes russet spotting on iceberg and romaine lettuce, and which specific enzyme is responsible?
A: Exposure to low concentrations of ethylene gas (C₂H₄), which upregulates phenylalanine ammonia-lyase, triggering brown pigment accumulation in the midribs.

Q: What ethylene-producing commodities must be kept away from lettuce in storage?
A: Ripening apples, pears, avocados, and bananas; propane-powered forklift exhaust can also trigger russet spotting.

Q: Is russet spotting reversible, and how should affected heads be handled?
A: No — the damage is irreversible. Affected heads must be removed and discarded or repurposed; they cannot be recovered.

Q: What causes tipburn in lettuce, and which nutrient is deficient?
A: A localized calcium deficiency in rapidly expanding leaf tissues; during rapid growth the transpiration stream cannot move calcium fast enough to the tips of forming leaves, causing cell walls to collapse.

Q: Why is tipburn dangerous even though it is not an infectious disease?
A: The necrotic dead tissue it creates provides a compromised entry point and nutrient source for secondary bacterial soft rots.

Q: Which Penicillium species causes blue mold in apples versus citrus?
A: Penicillium expansum primarily attacks apples and pears; Penicillium italicum and Penicillium digitatum dominate decay in citrus fruits.

Q: What is the definitive late-stage appearance of blue mold?
A: Dense, powdery blue or blue-green spore masses at the center of the lesion, bordered by a narrow advancing band of white mycelium, with a strong earthy or musty odor.

Q: Can Penicillium infect healthy, undamaged fruit skin?
A: No — it generally cannot penetrate a healthy waxy cuticle; infection almost exclusively requires mechanical wounds, stem punctures, insect stings, or impact bruises.

Q: Why is it dangerous to carelessly toss a moldy apple into a trash bin?
A: The dry, lightweight blue-green spores aerosolize and settle on adjacent display surfaces and healthy fruit; any fruit with micro-abrasions or stem punctures will rapidly become infected.

Q: What fungi cause anthracnose on tropical fruit, and which commodities are most affected?
A: Colletotrichum gloeosporioides and Colletotrichum asianum; primary commodities are mangoes, avocados, bell peppers, and papayas.

Q: What is the definitive visual marker of late-stage anthracnose that confirms Colletotrichum?
A: Gelatinous or powdery spore masses that are characteristically bright pink, salmon, or vivid orange in color, erupting from sunken necrotic lesions.

Q: Why can avocados or mangoes look perfect on receiving but erupt in black lesions three days later on the floor?
A: Colletotrichum establishes dormant (quiescent) infections in the field; as the fruit ripens and its natural antifungal compounds degrade, the dormant fungus reactivates and rapidly colonizes the flesh.

Q: How quickly does anthracnose destroy marketable flesh once latency breaks at full ripeness?
A: Within 2 to 4 days.

Q: What inventory practice is most critical for managing anthracnose losses on tropical fruit?
A: Strict FIFO (First-In, First-Out) rotation — older, ripening fruit must be moved to the front of the display so it sells before dormant infections have time to visually manifest.

Q: What does a shipment with an unusually high rapid anthracnose breakdown rate indicate about the supply chain?
A: A failure in pre-harvest field fungicide management or inadequate postharvest hot-water treatments at the packinghouse; the produce manager should aggressively seek credit from the supplier.

Q: What temperature causes chilling injury in tomatoes, making them vulnerable to Alternaria black mold?
A: Below 50°F — cold storage destroys cellular defenses without immediate visual damage; Alternaria colonizes the weakened tissue once tomatoes return to ambient display temperature.

Q: Where does Alternaria black mold typically initiate on a tomato?
A: At the stem scar, inside growth cracks, or in areas weakened by sunburn or chilling injury.

Q: What is the visual progression of Alternaria on carrots?
A: Greenish-brown, water-soaked lesions on leaf margins that expand into large, dark brown to black, irregularly shaped necrotic areas with a yellow halo; eventually the foliage scorches and the carrot roots develop a dry, black decay.

Q: What Fusarium species causes basal rot in onions and garlic, and where does infection begin?
A: Fusarium oxysporum; it typically initiates at the basal plate — the hardened bottom of the bulb where the roots attach — or at insect wound sites.

Q: What is the distinctive color diagnostic of Fusarium mycelium inside decayed bulb or tuber tissue?
A: While often white, Fusarium mycelium frequently shows diagnostic hues of pink, yellow, or reddish-purple.

Q: How slow is Fusarium dry rot, and what catastrophic secondary problem can it trigger?
A: Extremely slow — developing over weeks or months; the cracks and epidermal damage it creates become open entry points for bacterial soft rots, which can rapidly liquefy the entire bulb if humidity increases or the product gets wet.

Q: What storage conditions are required to prevent Fusarium-damaged onions and potatoes from collapsing to bacterial rot?
A: Strictly dry, well-ventilated bulk storage bins.

Q: Under retail food codes, how must spoiled produce be physically handled in the store?
A: It must be physically segregated from safe food, clean equipment, utensils, and single-service articles; placing actively rotting product on top of fresh greens in a cooler is a direct regulatory violation.

Q: How often must food contact surfaces in a continuously active produce prep room be fully cleaned and sanitized?
A: At least every 4 hours.

Q: What is the correct sequence for FIFO restocking on a produce display?
A: Pull all older product off the display; clean the surface of organic debris (loose skins, wet leaves, leaked juices); stock fresh new product at the base; place the older still-viable product on top so it sells first.

Q: What is the distinction between cosmetically challenged produce and actively decaying produce for handling purposes?
A: Cosmetically blemished but physiologically sound produce (wind scarring, limb rub, irregular shapes) can be used in secondary programs like cut fruit or imperfect bins; actively decaying produce exhibiting mold or soft rot must be destroyed.
