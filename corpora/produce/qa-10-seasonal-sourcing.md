Q: What percentage of U.S. leafy greens does Yuma, Arizona produce during winter months?
A: Yuma produces approximately 90 percent of the leafy greens consumed in the United States and Canada from November through April.

Q: How many pounds of lettuce does Yuma process per day at peak season?
A: Yuma processes upwards of 18 million pounds of lettuce per day at the peak of the season.

Q: When does Yuma take over leafy green production from Salinas, and when does it hand back?
A: Yuma runs from November through April. When summer heat makes Yuma inhospitable for delicate greens, production migrates back to the Salinas Valley in California.

Q: What triggers a sudden price spike in iceberg, romaine, and mixed greens?
A: Any disruption to the Salinas-to-Yuma transition — such as unseasonal frost in Yuma or extended rains in Salinas — immediately creates supply gaps, driving prices up abruptly.

Q: What share of U.S. vegetables does California produce, and what share of fruits and nuts?
A: California produces over a third of the nation's vegetables and two-thirds of its fruits and nuts.

Q: What makes the San Joaquin Valley unusual for crops like carrots and table grapes?
A: Its sandy soils and consistently mild spring and fall weather give it two distinct harvest seasons for those crops.

Q: Which U.S. state dominates domestic citrus production, and what is its global rank for oranges?
A: Florida dominates domestic citrus and ranks as the second-largest producer of oranges globally, behind Brazil.

Q: What role do Texas sweet onions play in the annual onion supply chain?
A: Texas sweet onions, harvested in the Rio Grande Valley in early spring, serve as the bridge between winter storage onions and the later Georgia Vidalia and Washington Walla Walla onion harvests.

Q: What percentage of U.S. avocado imports does Mexico supply?
A: Mexico supplies roughly 90 percent of all U.S. avocado imports, maintaining near year-round volume.

Q: When do Mexico's peak table grape exports occur, and why does that timing matter?
A: Mexico's peak table grape exports occur in May, June, and July, bridging the supply gap between the end of South American imports and the start of the California harvest.

Q: How many boxes of table grapes does Chile export annually, and what has caused that number to shrink?
A: Chile exports an estimated 60 million boxes, down significantly due to shifting climatic conditions and chronic droughts in regions like Valparaiso.

Q: Which country has surpassed Chile as the dominant winter grape supplier to the U.S.?
A: Peru has surpassed Chile, leveraging a warmer coastal climate and government investment in agricultural infrastructure.

Q: When does Peru's heaviest grape shipment window hit, and why does that window matter to retailers?
A: Peru ships heavily into December, targeting the lucrative holiday demand window.

Q: What pest-prevention protocol must Peruvian grapes undergo before entering the U.S.?
A: They must undergo strict cold-treatment protocols to prevent the entry of the Mediterranean fruit fly.

Q: What specialty vegetables does Guatemala supply to the U.S. during the North American winter?
A: Guatemala supplies snow peas, sugar snap peas, French beans, baby squash, and cantaloupes, exporting nearly 180 million pounds of vegetables annually.

Q: Which trade agreement supports Guatemalan agricultural exports to the U.S.?
A: The Dominican Republic-Central America Free Trade Agreement (CAFTA-DR).

Q: Why must Spanish clementines undergo cold treatment before entering the U.S.?
A: USDA APHIS mandates the cold treatment to eradicate the Mediterranean fruit fly (Medfly) before the fruit can enter the U.S. distribution network.

Q: How many cartons of citrus did South Africa export in its record season, and what types dominate?
A: South Africa moved nearly 158.7 million cartons, predominantly navel oranges, valencias, lemons, and grapefruit.

Q: How many tonnes of stone fruit does South Africa export annually?
A: Approximately 350,000 tonnes of plums, peaches, nectarines, and apricots.

Q: Why has competition for South African stone fruit increased recently, pushing up U.S. off-season prices?
A: A new bilateral trade protocol granting South African stone fruit zero-tariff access into China has increased global demand, forcing U.S. importers to compete more fiercely for those commodities.

Q: What is the difference between climacteric and non-climacteric crops?
A: Non-climacteric crops (citrus, strawberries, grapes, bell peppers) only ripen while attached to the parent plant and must be picked at peak maturity. Climacteric crops (bananas, avocados, tomatoes, peaches) can ripen after harvest by producing a surge of ethylene gas.

Q: What temperature are bananas held at during shipping to arrest ripening?
A: Strictly between 13–14°C (56–58°F).

Q: What concentration of ethylene gas is used to ripen bananas, and for how long at what temperature?
A: 100–150 ppm of ethylene for 24 to 48 hours at 15–20°C (59–68°F).

Q: What chemical changes occur inside a banana during ripening?
A: Starch hydrolyzes into sugars, pectin substances volatilize to soften the pulp, and organic acids develop the characteristic flavor profile.

Q: Is there a meaningful quality difference between naturally ripened and ethylene-gassed bananas?
A: Blind sensory panels have shown that while minute differences in volatile aroma compounds may exist, overall eating quality, texture, and sweetness are indistinguishable.

Q: What is a Stage 5 banana, and why is it the most important stage for retail display?
A: Stage 5 is yellow with green tips — the most popular retail stage, indicating the fruit is ready for imminent consumption.

Q: At what stage should bananas ideally be shipped from the distribution center to the retail backroom?
A: Stage 3 — 50% green / 50% yellow — to maximize shelf life on the retail floor.

Q: What stage is a banana at Stage 7, and what is it best used for?
A: Stage 7 is yellow flecked with brown, has the highest sugar content, and is ideal for baking or processing; it is highly perishable.

Q: How does temperature manipulation at the distribution center control banana ripening speed?
A: Operators drop the ambient air temperature from 64°F on Day 1 down to 58°F by Day 6 to slow respiration and hit the exact color stage requested by the retailer.

Q: Why can't avocados simply be ripened in standard banana rooms?
A: Avocados are highly exothermic — they generate immense latent heat as they ripen — and standard refrigeration equipment may lack the capacity to remove that excess heat quickly enough, leading to cooked, discolored, or unevenly ripened fruit.

Q: What core temperature and ethylene concentration are used to ripen avocados?
A: The core pulp temperature must be raised to 65–68°F (18–20°C) before applying approximately 100 ppm of ethylene.

Q: How does the harvest season affect avocado ethylene exposure time?
A: Early-season avocados with lower dry matter may require 36–48 hours of ethylene exposure, while mature late-season fruit with high oil content may only need 12–24 hours.

Q: What humidity level is required in avocado ripening rooms, and why?
A: 90–95% humidity, to prevent the rough skin from shriveling.

Q: What happens if CO₂ exceeds 1 percent in an avocado ripening room?
A: The CO₂ paralyzes the fruit's ethylene receptors, causing "checkerboard" ripening where some fruits in the same pallet turn soft and black while adjacent fruits stay rock hard.

Q: How often must avocado ripening rooms be vented, and why?
A: Every 12 hours, to prevent CO₂ buildup from exceeding the 1 percent threshold that blocks ethylene receptors.

Q: What is the Q₁₀ temperature coefficient, and why does it matter for produce?
A: It is the physical law that the rate of biological respiration drops drastically with every 10°C reduction in temperature. A one-hour delay in cooling summer-harvested produce can reduce its total retail shelf life by a full day.

Q: Why is "room cooling" — simply placing warm pallets in a cold room — ineffective?
A: The exterior boxes can freeze while the interior core of the pallet stays warm for hours or days, causing internal rot, condensation, and massive shrinkage.

Q: How does forced-air cooling work?
A: Pallets are aligned in a cooling tunnel, covered with a heavy plastic tarp, and exhaust fans create a negative static pressure zone that forces high-velocity refrigerated air directly through packaging ventilation holes, stripping heat rapidly and uniformly.

Q: What is hydrocooling and which crops is it used for?
A: Produce is showered with or submerged in chilled water, which transfers heat far more efficiently than air. It is only viable for crops that tolerate getting wet, such as cherries, carrots, or sweet corn.

Q: How does vacuum cooling work, and how fast does it chill a head of iceberg lettuce?
A: A vacuum pump depressurizes a sealed chamber, dropping the boiling point of water so that a microscopic fraction of the lettuce's internal moisture evaporates at room temperature, flashing the heat out of the tissue. It can chill the core of a dense head of iceberg lettuce to 34°F in under 30 minutes.

Q: What is the FIFO principle in distribution center cold storage?
A: First In, First Out — older product must be rotated to the front and sold before newer stock.

Q: Why must ethylene-producing crops be stored separately from ethylene-sensitive crops?
A: Ambient ethylene from producers like apples or avocados will cause sensitive crops like broccoli, celery, and lettuce to rapidly yellow, turn limp, and emit off-odors, rendering them unsellable.

Q: What is the optimal storage temperature and humidity for berries like blueberries and blackberries?
A: -0.5°C to 0°C (31–32°F) at 90–95% relative humidity, with a shelf life of 2 days to 2 weeks.

Q: What are the storage requirements for leafy greens like broccoli and bok choy?
A: 0°C (32°F) at 95–100% humidity, with a shelf life of 10 to 21 days.

Q: At what temperature and humidity should unripened avocados be stored?
A: 4°C to 13°C (40–55°F) at 85–95% humidity, with a shelf life of 2 to 8 weeks depending on variety.

Q: What do temperature data loggers like Sensitech TempTale or ColdMark detect?
A: They track cumulative temperature exposure during transit and trigger irreversible visual alarms if temperature thresholds are breached.

Q: What does it mean if a pallet arrives with a warm center but cold exterior boxes?
A: The data loggers will confirm the product was loaded onto the trailer before being properly pre-cooled at the farm.

Q: What is "short-cycling" in a refrigerated trailer, and how do you recognize it on product?
A: Short-cycling occurs when pallets are stacked too high or block airflow, causing the trailer's cold discharge air to hit top product directly. It appears as "top-freezing" — dark, wilted, translucent, or slimy leaves strictly on the top layer of boxes.

Q: Why does frozen plant tissue collapse into rapid decay once it thaws?
A: When plant tissue drops below its specific freezing point, the internal cellular emulsion destabilizes and bursts; while it may look intact while frozen, it decays rapidly upon thawing.

Q: What is the "Super Peso" and how did it affect U.S. produce prices?
A: Between late 2022 and early 2024, the Mexican peso rallied dramatically — dropping from 25 pesos per dollar down to roughly 16.33. This forced U.S. importers to spend more dollars for the same volume of Mexican avocados, berries, and tomatoes, raising retail prices.

Q: What drove the Mexican peso's extraordinary strength during this period?
A: High Mexican interest rates implemented by Banxico to combat inflation attracted vast foreign capital via "carry-trade" investments, further strengthening the currency.

Q: What is the H-2A program, and why does it raise produce prices?
A: H-2A is a temporary agricultural worker visa program that domestic growers rely on when local labor is scarce. It is more expensive than regular domestic labor, permanently raising the floor for retail produce prices.

Q: How long must land be managed organically before crops can legally carry the USDA organic seal?
A: 36 full months (3 years).

Q: What happens to a farmer's economics during the 36-month organic transition?
A: They bear all the higher input costs and reduced yields of organic farming but cannot legally command the organic price premium, creating a financial "death valley."

Q: What is the "Certified Transitional" label, and who pioneered it?
A: A tiered, audited certification for farms actively in their 36-month transition to organic, pioneered by Quality Assurance International (QAI). It allows farmers to command a modest price premium and stay financially solvent during the waiting period.

Q: What is the USDA Organic Transition Initiative (OTI), and how much funding does it carry?
A: A $300 million multi-agency effort to support farmers transitioning to organic, including the Risk Management Agency's TOGA program providing crop insurance premium assistance.

Q: How wide must buffer zones be between organic and conventional farmland?
A: There is no federally mandated width, but certifiers typically require 25 to over 100 feet depending on the risk of aerial spray or wind drift. Crops grown within the buffer zone cannot be sold as organic.

Q: What is FSMA Rule 204 (the Food Traceability Rule) and when did strict enforcement begin?
A: An FDA rule mandating digital traceability for high-risk fresh produce; strict compliance enforcement began January 2026.

Q: Which produce items appear on the FSMA Rule 204 Food Traceability List (FTL)?
A: Leafy greens, melons, tomatoes, sprouts, fresh herbs, cucumbers, peppers, and fresh-cut fruits, among others.

Q: What is a Critical Tracking Event (CTE) under FSMA 204?
A: Specific, actionable points in the supply chain where data must be captured, including Harvesting, Cooling, Initial Packing, Shipping, Receiving, and Transformation (such as chopping whole heads into bagged salad).

Q: What is a Traceability Lot Code (TLC)?
A: A unique alphanumeric descriptor assigned to a batch of raw agricultural commodities when first packed or received by a land-based facility, serving as the indelible digital anchor for that harvest lot through the entire supply chain.

Q: How quickly must supply chain entities provide traceability records to the FDA during an outbreak?
A: Within 24 hours — as a fully sortable electronic spreadsheet containing all relevant CTEs and Key Data Elements (KDEs).

Q: What is the practical floor-level benefit of FSMA 204 during a produce recall?
A: Instead of pulling the entire produce department, the digital traceability architecture allows surgical precision — isolating the exact Traceability Lot Code from the specific farm of origin so only the affected product is removed.

Q: What industry framework aligns with FSMA 204 to standardize how traceability data flows alongside physical pallets?
A: The Produce Traceability Initiative (PTI), which uses GS1 standards, Global Trade Item Numbers (GTINs), and Electronic Product Code Information Services (EPCIS).
