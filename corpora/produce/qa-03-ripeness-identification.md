Q: What is the difference between climacteric and non-climacteric fruits?
A: Climacteric fruits continue to ripen after harvest via a spike in ethylene production and respiration; non-climacteric fruits do not get sweeter or improve in flavor once separated from the plant and must be harvested at peak maturity.

Q: What temperature range causes chilling injury in tropical and subtropical produce?
A: Temperatures between 7°C (45°F) and 13°C (55°F) can cause chilling injury in susceptible crops, though the exact threshold varies by commodity.

Q: When do chilling injury symptoms typically become visible?
A: Symptoms rarely appear during cold storage; they manifest visibly and aggressively when the product returns to ambient retail display temperatures.

Q: What are the three senses in the "Three-Sense Triad" protocol for assessing ripeness?
A: Visual, tactile, and olfactory assessment.

Q: What percentage of light in the 650-nanometer range does a ripe Honeycrisp apple reflect?
A: 68% to 72%.

Q: What does a fermented or cider-like odor on an apple indicate?
A: Internal decay or extreme over-maturity.

Q: Why is storing apples near bananas or leafy greens a problem?
A: Apples release large quantities of ethylene gas, which rapidly accelerates the senescence and spoilage of ethylene-sensitive neighbors like bananas and leafy greens.

Q: When should an apple be culled entirely to the compost bin rather than rerouted to value-added?
A: Only when the flesh is deeply punctured, heavily bruised, or exhibiting visible fungal growth.

Q: Why are pears intentionally harvested before they are fully ripe?
A: Leaving them on the tree to soften results in a gritty, unpleasant texture; they must finish ripening off the tree.

Q: Which pear varieties do not change color as they ripen?
A: Bosc, Anjou, and Concorde.

Q: What is the "Check the Neck" method for pears?
A: Apply gentle thumb pressure near the stem end (the narrowest part); if the neck yields slightly, the pear is perfectly ripe and sweet.

Q: Why is squeezing the middle or bottom of a pear harmful?
A: Pears are highly susceptible to pressure damage, so squeezing the wide body immediately bruises the flesh and creates rapid decay and shrink.

Q: How should a customer ripen a hard pear at home?
A: Keep it in a loosely closed paper bag on the counter for about three days; the trapped natural gases speed up ripening.

Q: What should a customer do once a pear's neck yields to thumb pressure?
A: Put it straight into the refrigerator immediately; cold locks it at that exact level of ripeness so it doesn't get mushy.

Q: Are cherries climacteric or non-climacteric?
A: Non-climacteric; their sugar levels are fixed at the moment of harvest and they must be purchased fully ripe.

Q: What color must the background skin near the stem cavity of stone fruit show to indicate ripeness?
A: It must shift from green to a warm yellow or orange hue.

Q: What is the most catastrophic mistake a consumer can make with unripe peaches or plums?
A: Placing them in a cold refrigerator, which permanently halts ripening; the fruit will subsequently suffer chilling injury, resulting in a dry, mealy, flavorless interior.

Q: What stone fruit condition makes it ideal for value-added fresh-cut fruit cups?
A: Fruit that is too soft for a visually appealing bulk display but remains free of fungal mold.

Q: When must stone fruit be culled entirely to shrink?
A: When fungal fuzz, weeping juice, or sour fermentation is detected.

Q: What is the lowest safe storage temperature for Hass avocados?
A: 5°C (41°F).

Q: How does the avocado "stem button" method work?
A: Flick the small stem nub off the top; bright green underneath means perfectly ripe, brown underneath means overripe, and if the button won't budge the fruit is underripe.

Q: Why should clerks use the stem button test on only two or three avocados per shipment rather than individual floor fruit?
A: Removing the stem breaks the biological seal, dramatically accelerating rot; testing every fruit on the floor would destroy the display.

Q: What handling mistake do customers make most often with avocados?
A: Squeezing with fingertips or thumbs, which instantly bruises the delicate internal flesh and creates dark, unappetizing spots.

Q: What is a "Stage 5" avocado and what is it best used for?
A: Very soft, bordering on mushy — unsuitable for slicing but at the absolute pinnacle for mashing, making it ideal for store-made guacamole.

Q: When should an avocado be culled to total loss?
A: If the flesh yields with zero resistance, feels hollow, or the stem area shows visible white mold.

Q: What does fine, uniform brown speckling on a banana peel indicate?
A: Localized cellular potassium ion leakage and maximum sugar content — the banana is at peak sweetness.

Q: What do large, coalescing black patches that appear sunken on a banana indicate?
A: Cellular collapse and over-maturity.

Q: At what temperature do bananas suffer chilling injury?
A: Below 13°C (56°F).

Q: What happens when a banana is refrigerated before it is ripe?
A: Cold exposure permanently halts the ripening enzymes and causes the peel to oxidize, turning a dull gray or black.

Q: Why must bananas never be displayed directly next to ripe apples or melons?
A: Because bananas are highly ethylene-sensitive, proximity to heavy ethylene producers will accelerate their over-ripening.

Q: What is the best value-added use for heavily speckled bananas?
A: Bag and mark down as "Baking Bananas," or peel, freeze, and use in in-store juice bars and smoothie programs.

Q: Why can't a mango's ripeness be judged by skin color alone?
A: Different cultivars (e.g., Tommy Atkins vs. Honey/Ataulfo) may be red, golden yellow, or entirely green when perfectly ripe.

Q: What is the lowest safe storage temperature for unripe mangoes?
A: 10°C (50°F); below this, chilling injury causes grayish discoloration, uneven ripening, and flavor degradation.

Q: Where on a mango should you smell for olfactory ripeness assessment?
A: At the stem end, where the fruit was attached to the tree.

Q: Why will a pineapple on the counter never get sweeter after purchase?
A: Pineapples are non-climacteric; their sugar content is completely fixed at harvest and will not increase off the plant.

Q: Where on a pineapple should you smell to assess ripeness, and what volatile compound is being detected?
A: Smell the base of the fruit, not the crown; the dominant volatile is ethyl butanoate, detectable at thresholds as low as 0.003 ppm.

Q: What does a sharp acetone or sour note at a pineapple's base indicate?
A: The sugars have begun to ferment.

Q: Is pulling a leaf from a pineapple's crown a reliable ripeness test?
A: No — it is a complete myth; a loose leaf only indicates the crown is drying out or beginning to rot, not internal sugar content.

Q: What is the lowest safe storage temperature for pineapples?
A: 7°C (45°F); below this, chilling injury causes dull green skin and internal browning.

Q: What is the lowest safe storage temperature for papayas?
A: 7°C (45°F); below this, cold stress causes surface pitting, failure to ripen, off-flavors, and rapid decay.

Q: What is the lowest safe storage temperature for tomatoes?
A: 10°C (50°F); below this, chilling injury permanently halts volatile flavor compound production, causes grainy texture, and creates sunken surface pitting.

Q: What volatile compound does a perfectly ripe tomato emit at the stem scar, and at what threshold?
A: Hexanal, detectable at 0.08 ppm; it provides a clean, green-leafy, earthy scent.

Q: What does a musty or damp odor at a tomato's stem scar indicate?
A: Internal fungal colonization.

Q: What is blossom end rot on a tomato and what causes it?
A: A dark, sunken lesion on the bottom of the fruit caused by pre-harvest calcium deficiency.

Q: Why is citrus a non-climacteric fruit category?
A: Oranges, lemons, limes, and grapefruits do not accumulate any additional sugars or improve in internal quality once harvested; they must reach peak maturity on the tree.

Q: What is the single most reliable indicator of high juice content in a citrus fruit?
A: The fruit feeling exceedingly firm and notably heavy for its relative size.

Q: What causes Penicillium mold (blue-green fuzz) on citrus stored in the refrigerator?
A: Sealing citrus in airtight plastic bags creates condensation and a humid microclimate that provides the perfect breeding ground for the mold.

Q: What does a watermelon's ground spot indicate, and what color should it be?
A: The ground spot is where the melon rested on soil during growth; a deep, creamy yellow color indicates it ripened on the vine. A bright white spot means it was harvested immaturely.

Q: What is a "full slip" on a cantaloupe and why does it matter?
A: A smooth, slightly indented stem scar indicating the melon separated cleanly and naturally from the vine at full physiological maturity; it confirms the cantaloupe was harvested at the right time.

Q: Where on a cantaloupe should you smell for ripeness assessment?
A: At the blossom end (opposite the stem scar); a highly perfumed, musky, sweet aroma confirms ripeness.

Q: Why is the acoustic "thump" test for watermelon unreliable?
A: It is highly subjective and far less accurate than examining the physical reality of the yellow ground spot.

Q: Are all commercial berries climacteric or non-climacteric?
A: All commercial berries (strawberries, blueberries, blackberries, raspberries) are non-climacteric; their sugar levels are fixed at harvest and they must be purchased fully ripe.

Q: What does the dusty white "bloom" on a blueberry indicate?
A: It is a naturally occurring epicuticular wax that protects the fruit; its presence is a definitive indicator of freshness and minimal handling.

Q: How should a clerk evaluate the aroma of strawberries in a cold clamshell?
A: Hold the clamshell in the palms for 10 seconds to warm the fruit slightly before sniffing; cold temperatures suppress the volatility of aromatic compounds.

Q: What pathogen causes gray mold rot in berries, and what triggers its rapid spread?
A: Botrytis cinerea; introducing excess moisture to the berry skin (such as washing before refrigerating) dramatically accelerates its growth, potentially consuming an entire container within 24 hours.

Q: What ripeness cue indicates green grapes are mature?
A: A slight, translucent amber blush on green varieties indicates high sugar maturity.

Q: What is "berry shatter" in grapes and what causes it?
A: The physiological dropping of fruit from the stem, directly caused by water loss and rough handling.

Q: How can you tell a bunch of grapes is fresh versus old from the stems?
A: Fresh grapes have bright green, pliable, flexible stems; aged grapes have brown, dry, and brittle stems from oxidation and moisture loss.

Q: What does the "milk stage" test on corn involve?
A: Pierce a single kernel with a thumbnail; milky opaque liquid means peak sweetness, clear watery liquid means immature, no liquid or a dry/doughy kernel means overripe and starchy.

Q: What visual cues indicate ripe corn without opening the husk?
A: Bright green husks tightly wrapped around the cob, dark brown and completely dried-out silk at the top, and a blunt (not pointed) tip at the end of the cob.

Q: Why must sweet corn be refrigerated immediately after purchase?
A: The moment corn is severed from the stalk, its sugars begin converting to starches; heat drastically accelerates this, meaning a sweet ear can become bland and starchy within hours at room temperature.

Q: What are the six steps of the leafy greens "crisping" protocol in the correct order?
A: (1) Sanitize the sink and tools; (2) trim a small portion off the stem base to re-open vascular tissue; (3) fill the sink with tepid room-temperature water; (4) fully submerge for a minimum of 45 minutes; (5) remove, drain, then apply rubber bands after draining; (6) cold shock in a cooler for at least two hours.

Q: Why must the crisping water be room temperature rather than cold or hot?
A: Cold water constricts the plant's vascular tissue and prevents capillary water uptake; hot water irreparably damages and cooks the delicate epidermal cells of the leaves.

Q: Why must rubber bands not be applied to leafy greens before soaking?
A: As the plant tissue absorbs water and expands, the band causes the cellular ribs to snap against it, creating micro-fractures that lead to premature rusting and accelerated shrink.

Q: What effect does ethylene gas have on leafy greens?
A: It acts as a senescence hormone, causing rapid chlorophyll degradation (yellowing), rust-colored spotting on ribs, and accelerated wilting.

Q: What is the economic scale of produce shrink in supermarkets?
A: Produce shrink accounts for 16% of total supermarket shrink, translating to a loss of 4% to 8% of the produce department's total gross sales.

Q: What are the three operational streams for categorizing produce during daily floor culls?
A: (1) Prime floor display — flawless aesthetics and optimal firmness; (2) value-added reroute — superficial defects that don't compromise internal quality; (3) shrink/compost — fungal rot, active fermentation, weeping fluids, or severe chilling injury.

Q: Why does fresh-cut produce require stricter management than whole fruit?
A: Fresh-cut produce respires at a significantly higher rate than intact fruit, drastically shortening its viable shelf life, requiring rigorous FIFO rotation, strict date-coding, and continuous temperature monitoring.

Q: What is the lowest safe storage temperature for cucumbers and bell peppers?
A: Both have a lowest safe storage temperature of 10°C (50°F) for cucumbers and 7°C (45°F) for bell peppers.

Q: What is "sheet pitting" on bell peppers and what causes it?
A: Large, sunken areas on the pepper skin caused by chilling injury from storage below 7°C (45°F).

Q: Why are English greenhouse cucumbers wrapped in shrink plastic, and what happens if the seal is broken early?
A: The plastic maintains humidity around the thin, highly water-loss-susceptible skin; breaking the seal prematurely destroys their shelf life.

Q: How does ethylene gas damage cucumbers stored near apples or tomatoes?
A: It dramatically accelerates yellowing of the cucumber skin and causes rapid tissue decay.
